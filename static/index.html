    Cold call — No website

    For prospects with a Google listing but no site. Lead with money they're losing, not the absence.

    1. Opener 10 sec

    "Hey, is the owner around? Tell them it's [Your name] from [Your company] — I'll be quick."

    No "How are you today?" No "Got a minute?" — those signal cold-caller. "I'll be quick" earns 30 seconds.

    2. Hook 15 sec

    "Hey [Name], real quick — I help local [contractors / salons / restaurants] in [your city] book more jobs from Google Maps. I noticed you've got reviews and a phone number on Google but no website tied to your listing — which means when someone Googles you, a chunk of them just bounce because there's nothing for them to look at. I help fix that. Got 30 seconds for me to explain how, or want me to text you the details?"

    Specific observation = you actually looked. Two-option close = both easy yeses.

    3. Discovery 60 sec, only if they bite

    "Where do most of your jobs come from right now? Word of mouth, Google, Facebook?"
    "Got it. And when a brand-new customer who's never met you wants to size you up before calling — what do they see?"

    Makes them feel the gap themselves. They'll usually answer "just my Google listing" — there's your opening.

    4. Offer

    "Here's what I'd do. I build you a one-page site — your name, your services, a few photos of your work, click-to-call button, your reviews pulled in from Google. Live in a week. Three-fifty bucks, hosting included for the first year. If a single new customer calls because of it, you've made the money back. Want me to send you a couple examples of ones I've done?"

    Low decision threshold. Concrete deliverable. ROI is obvious. Examples = next step.

    5. Objections click to expand

    "I get all my work from word of mouth."

    "That's awesome — that's the hard part, you've already done it. Right now those word-of-mouth leads are calling, but every one of them Googles you first. Half of them already have. The website's not for finding new customers — it's for closing the ones who heard about you and aren't sure yet."

    "I'm too busy."

    "That's why I do all of it. You'll spend maybe 15 minutes total — sending me your logo if you have one and looking at the draft. I write the copy, take care of everything else."

    "I had a website before, didn't help."

    "Was anyone updating it? Most of the ones I see were built five years ago, looked dated, didn't show up on phones. The whole game now is mobile and Google reviews — totally different than what you probably had."

    "How much?" (asked early, before value is set)

    "Depends what you need — I do everything from one-pagers at three-fifty up to full sites with online booking. Want me to take a quick look at your situation and get back to you with a number tomorrow?"

    "Just send me an email." (the brush-off)

    "Will do — what's the best email? While I'm at it, what's the one thing that would make this a yes for you? I'll build the proposal around that."

    Industry tweaks

    Salons: Swap "more jobs" for "more bookings." Lead with "Most salons I work with see 5–10 extra bookings a month from a website with online scheduling."

    Plumbers / HVAC: Lean on emergencies. "When someone's basement is flooding at 9pm, the first thing they do is Google plumber. The ones with a website + click-to-call get the call. The ones without get the next guy."

    Don't say

    • "Can I have a few minutes of your time?" — gets a no on autopilot.
    • "I'm just calling to check in." — empty calorie sentence.
    • "Do you have a website?" — you already know they don't, this insults.
    • "I noticed your business is missing X, Y, Z." — feels like criticism.
    • "We can offer you a great deal." — agency-speak, sounds like a scam.
    • Anything about SEO, rankings, conversions, impressions — they care about phone calls and bookings, not those words.

    Cold call — Existing website (outdated)

    For prospects with an old/non-mobile site. Blame the industry change, not them. Frame as "tune-up," not "rebuild."

    1. Opener 10 sec

    "Hey, is [owner] around? Tell him it's [Your name] from [Your company], I'll be quick."

    2. Hook — lead with respect 20 sec

    "Hey [Name] — quick reason for the call. I work with [contractors / dentists / law firms] around [your city]. I noticed you've actually got a website up, which puts you ahead of half the businesses in your area — most of them don't have anything. The reason I called is, the way Google ranks local sites changed pretty hard a few years back when most searches moved to phones, and a lot of really established businesses are losing visibility for reasons that have nothing to do with how good they are at what they do. I help fix that. Got 60 seconds for me to explain how it works?"

    Compliments their site. Blames Google, not them. "Established businesses" is implicit praise. "Has nothing to do with how good your business is" absolves them.

    3. The explanation

    "So when phones became the main way people search for local services, Google started ranking sites that load fast on mobile higher than ones built for desktop — even if the desktop site is great. That's the main shift. I help local businesses refresh their existing site so it plays nice with the new rules. Same business, same look-and-feel if you want it, just tuned up for phones and faster on Google. Most of my clients see their phone start ringing more within the first month or two."

    "Even if the desktop site is great" gives them an out. "Refresh" / "tune-up" instead of "rebuild." "Same look-and-feel" preserves their pride.

    4. Offer — two tiers

    "I do two versions depending on what you want. There's a tune-up — keep your current setup mostly intact, modernize the parts Google cares about, that runs around $400. Or a fuller refresh where I rebuild the whole thing fresh, mobile-first, click-to-call, your Google reviews pulled in automatically — that one's $700. Either way, way more affordable than what these used to cost. Want me to send you a couple examples of refreshes I've done in your industry, no strings?"

    Two tiers = bigger psychological yes than "do you want it or not." Cheaper than what they paid before is a relief, not a red flag.

    5. Objections click to expand

    "I just paid for that site."

    "Totally hear you, that always stings. Two questions if you don't mind — when did you have it built, and is the guy who built it still maintaining it for you?" (Almost always: a while ago, and no.) "Yeah, that's the most common situation. Web stuff has a shelf life that nobody warns you about — five years used to last forever, now it's more like two. Not your fault, just how the industry runs."

    "My site is fine, I get plenty of work."

    "Good — that means the business is solid. The question I'd want to answer is whether the site is helping you or just sitting there neutral. Have you been seeing as many first-time-customer calls as you used to, or is most of your work coming from people who already know you?" (Lets them tell you their own pain.)

    "How is yours different from what I have?"

    "Honestly, the underlying skills are the same — what's different is the toolkit. Modern site builders let me do in eight hours what used to take sixty, and the result loads twice as fast on phones. So you're getting current tech without paying current prices."

    "What was wrong with my old site?"

    "Nothing was wrong with it for the time it was built. Phones changed everything — sites that were good for desktop in 2015 just aren't speaking the same language Google does now. It's not a quality issue, it's a vintage issue."

    "Why are you cheaper than what I paid before?"

    "The hourly work is shorter. I'm not skipping anything — the tools are just better than they were when you bought your last one. Same way a plumber today does in a half hour what used to take two."

    "Send me a proposal."

    "Sure — give me 24 hours. Quick question to make the proposal sharper: what's the one thing on your current site that drives you crazy when you look at it?" (They'll always have an answer. Use it as the headline of your proposal.)

    The mockup gift (closes 30%+)

    Before the call, build a quick visual of how their refreshed site would look — same logo, same name, modernized layout. At the end of the call:

    "Tell you what — let me put together a quick visual showing how a refreshed version would look while keeping your branding. I'll text it over later today, no pressure, no follow-up unless you reach back out. Worst case you've got a free reference for whenever you do decide to update. Sound fair?"

    Framed as a gift, not "I already assumed you needed help." Owners who'd never spend $400 hypothetically will spend $400 to fix the version of their business they just saw.

    Tonal swaps — say this, not that

    • Not "your old site" → "your current site"
    • Not "outdated" → "from the desktop era" / "built before mobile took over"
    • Not "broken" → "due for a refresh"
    • Not "losing customers" → "missing some calls you'd otherwise be getting"
    • Not "your competitors are beating you" → "some businesses in your area refreshed last year and started showing up higher"
    • Not "rebuild" / "redo" → "tune-up" / "refresh" / "modernize"
    • Not "it hasn't kept up" → "the rules changed around it"

    Cold call — SEO Services

    For local businesses ranking on page 2-3 of Google. Lead with the lost calls, not the algorithm.

    1. Opener 10 sec

    "Hey, is the owner around? Tell them it's [Your name] from [Your company] — I'll be quick."

    Same rule as every cold call — no "How are you today?" Get past the gatekeeper by sounding like someone they already know.

    2. Hook 20 sec

    "Hey [Name], real quick — I help local [contractors / dentists / law firms] in [your city] show up higher when someone Googles their service. I just checked — when I search '[their service] near me' from your area, you come up on page two. That's basically invisible. Three of your competitors are above you and they're getting those calls. Got 30 seconds for me to explain what's costing you the ranking?"

    Specific search + specific result = they know you actually looked. "Three competitors above you" creates urgency without naming names yet.

    3. Discovery 60 sec, only if they bite

    "Where do most of your new customers come from right now — Google, referrals, or paid ads?"
    "Got it. And when someone Googles you or your service from scratch — no referral, just cold search — how often does the phone ring from that?"

    If they say "not much" — that IS the problem. If they say "a lot" — pivot to "we can multiply that."

    4. Offer — three things you fix

    "There are basically three reasons businesses get stuck on page two. One — Google Business Profile isn't optimized, missing categories, no recent posts, photos uploaded once five years ago. Two — your site is missing the service-area pages Google wants to see for each town you serve. Three — no one's pointing back to you, what we call backlinks. I fix all three. Most clients move from page two to top three within 90 days, and the first call usually pays for the whole thing."

    Three concrete reasons = sounds like a diagnosis, not a sales pitch. "First call pays for the whole thing" is the math owner-operators care about.

    5. Pricing

    "$500 one-time for the full optimization — Google Business Profile, the service-area pages, the initial backlink push. After that $200/month if you want me to keep posting fresh content and building links so you stay ahead. Most guys do month-to-month, no contract, cancel whenever. Sound reasonable?"

    One-time price removes "monthly contract" objection upfront. Optional monthly is recurring revenue without feeling forced.

    6. Objections click to expand

    "I already pay someone for SEO."

    "Cool, who are you working with? (Listen.) No knock on them — but if you're on page two right now, something's off. Mind if I do a free 5-minute audit and send it to you? If your current guy is doing it right, the audit will confirm it. If something's missing, you'll know either way. No charge, no follow-up unless you reach out."

    "SEO is too slow, I need leads now."

    "Totally fair. Honest answer — SEO takes 60-90 days to really move. But the Google Business Profile work I do shows up in 2-3 weeks for local searches. And it's free traffic once it's built. Ads stop the day you stop paying. SEO compounds. The two work great together — most of my clients run a little Google Ads while I build the SEO underneath, then they cut ads once organic kicks in."

    "Can you guarantee page one?"

    "Nope — and you should run from anyone who does. Google explicitly says no SEO is allowed to guarantee rankings. What I CAN guarantee is the work, and I can show you what page-one looks like for businesses I've worked with. Want me to text you three case studies? You can call them and ask."

    "I tried SEO before and it didn't work."

    "What did you try? (Listen.) Yeah, that's super common — most SEO 'packages' from a few years back were just monthly reporting on stuff that didn't move the needle. The game changed when Google started caring more about real local signals — actual reviews, fresh photos, content that matches what people search. The work I do is in that lane. Different game than what you tried."

    "I'm getting enough work already."

    "That's awesome — means the business is solid. The question I'd ask is: do you want to keep things at this level, or do you want optionality? Because the second you want to scale, raise prices, add a second crew, hire a salesperson — you'll need more inbound. Better to plant that tree now while business is good than to scramble when you need it."

    The "free audit" close

    If they won't commit on the call but seem warm:

    "Tell you what — let me run a free audit on your Google ranking, your competitors, and what's costing you the placement. I'll send it over later today as a one-pager. No pressure, no follow-up unless you reach out. If it's helpful and you want to fix it, we can talk. If not, you've got a free reference."

    Framing it as a gift, not "let me sell you." Owners who'd never spend $500 hypothetically will spend $500 when they see exactly what's broken on a one-pager.

    Tonal swaps — say this, not that

    • Not "you're not ranking" → "you're coming up on page two, which is basically invisible"
    • Not "your site is bad" → "Google's missing some signals it wants to see from you"
    • Not "we'll optimize your SEO" → "we'll fix the three things keeping you off page one"
    • Not "you need backlinks" → "nobody's pointing back to you yet"
    • Not "guaranteed page one" → "here's what page one has looked like for businesses we've worked with"
    • Not "SEO is a long game" → "first results in 2-3 weeks, full ranking in 60-90 days"

    Cold call — Graphic Design / Branding

    For businesses with a dated or DIY logo. Frame as "looking the part," not "your logo is bad."

    1. Opener 10 sec

    "Hey, is the owner around? Tell them it's [Your name] from [Your company] — I'll only take a minute."

    2. Hook 20 sec

    "Hey [Name], quick reason for the call. I help local [contractors / restaurants / fitness studios] in [your city] level up the way their brand looks online — logo, business cards, social media graphics, signage, that kind of thing. I came across your business and the work is clearly there, but the brand visuals haven't quite caught up to how big you've gotten. Got 30 seconds for me to explain what I'm seeing?"

    "The work is clearly there" is the compliment. "Hasn't caught up to how big you've gotten" reframes it as growth, not failure. They've outgrown their brand — that's flattering.

    3. Discovery 60 sec, only if they bite

    "Quick question — who designed your current logo and how long ago?"
    "Got it. And when you hand someone a business card or someone scrolls past your Instagram, what's the impression you want them to walk away with?"

    Almost always: "my brother-in-law / my cousin / I made it in Canva five years ago." Then the gap between current visuals and the impression they want is YOUR opening.

    4. Offer — two tiers

    "I do two versions. The essentials package is logo refresh plus business cards, color palette, and three social media templates — runs $400, takes about a week. The full brand kit is everything above plus signage layout, vehicle wrap mock-up, letterhead, email signature, and a brand guidelines doc you can hand to anyone else who designs for you — that's $900, about two weeks. Both come with unlimited revisions until you love it. Want me to text you a couple recent examples?"

    Two tiers > yes/no question. "Unlimited revisions" kills the biggest design objection ("what if I don't like it"). The "brand guidelines doc" is the upsell that makes the higher tier feel like an asset, not just more files.

    5. Objections click to expand

    "I already have a logo, I don't need a new one."

    "Totally — and a lot of my clients say the same thing at first. The question I'd ask is: do customers see it more on your truck, your shirt, your Instagram, your website? Because logos that look fine on a business card sometimes don't translate to a small Instagram thumbnail or a billboard. I can do a free quick mock-up showing how yours holds up across those formats. If it works, great, no need to change. If it doesn't, you'll see what I mean."

    "I made mine in Canva, it works fine."

    "Canva is great for getting started — no shame in that. The thing is, when you're trying to look like a $5 million business and you've got a Canva logo, customers subconsciously price you lower. Higher-end clients judge that stuff fast. Worth a $400 refresh if it lets you charge 10-20% more? That's usually the calculus."

    "How much for just a logo?"

    "Just a logo on its own is $250, but honestly I don't recommend it. A logo without matching business cards, social colors, and a font system means every time you make a flyer or social post it looks like a different company. The $400 essentials package fixes that. But if you really just want the logo, $250 works."

    "What if I don't like what you come up with?"

    "Built into the price. Unlimited revisions until you love it — I don't take it personally. About 80% of my clients land it in the second round. The 20% who don't, we just keep iterating. You don't pay the final piece until you're happy."

    "I'm not really a 'branding' guy, I just do the work."

    "Totally fair, and I respect that. Here's the thing though — your customers ARE branding-aware whether you are or not. When two contractors quote the same price but one's truck and uniform look sharp and the other looks slapped together, customers pick the sharp one. They can't help it. Branding for tradespeople isn't about ego, it's about closing more of your quotes."

    The free mock-up close

    Warm but uncommitted prospect — offer the visual:

    "Tell you what — let me put together a free quick logo refresh mock-up before any commitment. Same name, same vibe, just a modern take. I'll text it over later today, no follow-up unless you reach back out. If it makes your current one look dated, you'll know. If it doesn't, you've got free art."

    The visual sells itself. Once they see a clean version next to their current one, the gap is undeniable. Don't argue — show.

    Tonal swaps — say this, not that

    • Not "your logo is bad" → "the brand hasn't caught up to how big you've gotten"
    • Not "outdated" → "from your early days" / "the original version"
    • Not "amateur" → "DIY" / "Canva-built"
    • Not "you need a rebrand" → "a refresh that keeps your name and feel, just polished"
    • Not "professional design" → "the version of your brand that matches the price you want to charge"
    • Not "trust me, it'll look better" → "let me text you a quick mock-up, you decide"

    Cold call — Social Media Management

    For businesses with a stale Instagram or Facebook. Lead with the lost trust signal, not "you should post more."

    1. Opener 10 sec

    "Hey, is the owner around? Tell them it's [Your name] from [Your company] — I'll be quick."

    2. Hook 25 sec

    "Hey [Name], real quick — I help local [gyms / salons / restaurants / contractors] in [your city] turn their Instagram and Facebook from dead pages into actual lead sources. I checked yours — your last post is from [seven months ago]. That's not a knock — you're busy running the business. But here's what's happening: when a new customer is deciding between you and a competitor, they're checking your social, and a dead feed makes them wonder if you're still open.     Specific stale date = they know you actually looked. "Are you still open" is the killer line — most owners hadn't thought of that interpretation. Reframes "I should post more" as "I'm losing trust silently."

    3. Discovery 60 sec, only if they bite

    "What's the main reason you don't post more often — is it time, not knowing what to post, or just not a priority?"
    "Got it. And what's the goal when you DO post — getting new customers, staying top-of-mind with existing ones, or hiring?"

    Most owners say "time" or "I don't know what to post" — both are sellable. Goal question forces them to articulate what social SHOULD be doing for them. Their answer is your offer angle.

    4. Offer — done-for-you

    "Here's what I do. Twelve posts a month across Instagram and Facebook — mix of behind-the-scenes shots, customer wins, before/afters, the occasional promo. Most clients see their first new-customer DM within the first month. $400/month, no contract, cancel any time. Sound reasonable?"

    "Twelve posts" is specific = concrete. "I write the captions, design the graphics" = they don't have to think. "First new-customer DM within the first month" = measurable promise.

    5. Objections click to expand

    "My niece / my employee already does it."

    "That's awesome — keep them around for the behind-the-scenes stuff, they know the business. The thing I'd ask is: what's the engagement been like, and are you seeing actual new customers from it? Most owners who tell me 'we have someone' are paying the niece $200 for posts that get 8 likes. I do strategy, design, hashtag research, and respond to comments — different game than your niece scheduling posts. But if it's working, by all means keep it."

    "My customers don't use Instagram / Facebook."

    "Fair concern. Real answer — your existing customers might not, but new ones are checking. Anyone under 50 doing their first search for a [your service] in your area is going to Google you, find your social, and judge. Even if they don't follow you, the impression they form there decides whether they call. So social isn't a place to 'reach customers' — it's a place to be checked out. Different goal."

    "I don't have time to send you photos every month."

    "Cool, then we do a quarterly shoot — I spend two hours on-site, snap a few hundred shots of the work, the crew, the truck, the storefront — gives me three months of content. Most owners hate doing photos but love the result. We do it once, you don't think about it for 90 days."

    "$400/month is a lot."

    "Totally hear that. Let me ask — what's a customer worth to you? (Wait for number.) So if social brings in even one new [customer / job / member] a month, it pays for itself, right? Most clients see 2-4 new ones per month. Worst case scenario, you cancel after month one if it's not pulling weight. No contract."

    "I don't want to be on camera / 'cringe' / influencer-y."

    "Yeah, nobody wants that. Different lane — I focus on the work, not the owner. Before/after shots, the crew on site, time-lapses, customer reactions. You stay invisible if you want. The work IS the content. Influencer stuff is for influencers, not for businesses that get paid to deliver."

    The "free first week" close

    Warm but uncommitted — offer a sample:

    "Tell you what — I'll mock up your first week of posts before you commit a dollar. Three Instagram posts, three Facebook posts, captions and graphics, ready to publish. I'll send them over by Friday. If you love them, we go monthly. If they're not your vibe, you keep them and use them yourself, no charge. Sound fair?"

    Risk reversal. They get six free posts either way. The likelihood they keep working with you after seeing the quality is high — most owners can't believe how much better professional content looks compared to their phone shots.

    Tonal swaps — say this, not that

    • Not "you should post more" → "your dead feed makes customers wonder if you're still open"
    • Not "your social is bad" → "the social isn't matching what the business actually does"
    • Not "you need to be on TikTok" → "wherever your customers are checking you out — Instagram, Facebook — we make sure it looks alive"
    • Not "you need a content strategy" → "twelve posts a month, done for you"
    • Not "engagement is down" → "you're losing trust signals without realizing"
    • Not "you'll go viral" → "first new-customer DM usually within the first month"
    • Not "social media manager" → "I handle the social side so you don't have to think about it"

    3. Discovery 60 sec, only if they bite

    "What's your current setup look like — are you running the marketing yourself, do you have someone, or is it kind of nobody?"

    4. Offer — bundled, named, simple

    "Here's what I do. Think of it as your marketing department, outsourced. One flat monthly fee, no setup, no contract.     "Outsourced marketing department" sells the role, not the deliverables. The "$15/hr part-timer" anchor reframes $1,200 from "a lot" to "a steal." Always end with a tour question — "walk you through" is lower-stakes than "send a proposal."

    5. Objections click to expand

(Now you've narrowed it. Pitch only the gap.) "Cool — I can plug in just for that piece, no need to touch what's working. Half my clients started that way, then moved everything over once they saw the difference."

    "$1,200 a month is way too much."

    "Totally hear that. Let me ask — what's a customer worth to you? (Wait for number.) So if this brings you even [X] extra customers a month, it pays for itself. And just so you have the comparison — most owners I talk to are paying $400 for social, $300 for SEO, $200 for the web guy, and still feel like nothing's coordinated. I'm $1,200 doing all three and actually talking to each other. But — if budget's tight, we can start with just the piece that bugs you the most for $400-500 and grow from there. No pressure."

    "My wife / employee handles it."

    "That's awesome — keep them on the relationship side, they know the customers. Real question: is doing the marketing taking time away from something that pays better? (Wait.) Yeah, that's the thing — every hour your wife spends figuring out Canva is an hour she's not doing what she's actually great at. I take that off her plate. She still has the final say on everything that goes out."

    "I tried an agency before, didn't work."

    "Hear that a lot. Two questions if you don't mind — was it a big agency or a smaller one, and were you their biggest client or one of fifty? (Almost always: big, and one of fifty.) Yeah, that's the issue. The big agencies sign 50 local businesses, hand you off to a 22-year-old account manager, and you become a checkbox. I work with maybe 15 clients total, by design. You'd be talking to me directly, every time."

    "How long until I see results?"

    "Honest answer: social and reviews you'll see movement in the first 30 days — more eyeballs, more DMs, more phone calls. Website refresh shows up in your Google rankings around the 60-90 day mark. The marketing-as-a-system thing — where new customers just keep showing up without you thinking about it — that's usually a 4-6 month build. I tell you that upfront because most agencies promise overnight magic and then ghost you when it doesn't hit."

    "Send me a proposal."

    "Sure thing. Give me 24 hours — I'll put together something specific to your business, not a generic template. Quick question to make it sharper: out of everything we talked about, what would make this an easy yes for you?" (They'll tell you the lever. Lead your proposal with that.)

    "I'm too busy to onboard right now."

    "That's exactly why people hire me. Onboarding is one 30-minute call — I ask you about the business, your customers, what's working, what you hate. After that you don't think about marketing again unless you want to. Most clients say it actually frees up two or three hours a week they were spending on it themselves."

    The "marketing audit" close (closes 25%+)

    Soft yes, high-value, no commitment needed:

    "Tell you what — let me put together a free 5-minute marketing audit for your business. I'll record a quick Loom walking through your website, your Google profile, your social, and your top three competitors. Tell you exactly what's working and what's leaking customers — even if you never hire me. I'll send it over by Friday. Sound fair?"

    Costs you 15 minutes. The Loom does 80% of the selling for you because they're SEEING the problems instead of being told about them. Half the people who agree to the audit hire you within two weeks.

    Industry tweaks

    Contractors / home services: Lead with the "$10,000 job, $1,200/month investment" math. "One signed job pays you back for 8 months of working with me."

    Med spas / dental / cosmetic: Lean into reviews and before/afters. "Your industry lives and dies on Google reviews and visible results — both of those are 80% of what I do."

    Restaurants / hospitality: Reframe around foot traffic and repeat customers. "Marketing for restaurants isn't ads — it's making sure people thinking about dinner on a Wednesday see your food before they see anyone else's."

    Local services with no website: Drop the price floor to $800/month and bundle the new website into month one as a "no extra setup fee" sweetener.

    Tonal swaps — say this, not that

    • Not "we offer marketing services" → "your marketing department, outsourced"
    • Not "full-stack agency" → "one person, one bill, the whole thing"
    • Not "synergy / strategy / brand" → "your website, your Google, your social — coordinated"
    • Not "we'll grow your business" → "more phone calls, more DMs, more booked jobs"
    • Not "performance-based pricing" → "flat monthly, cancel any time"
    • Not "marketing audit" (when explaining cost) → "a Loom walkthrough of what's leaking customers"
    • Not "you need branding" → "the parts your customers see should feel like they belong together"
    • Not "I have packages" → "one flat fee, no setup, no contract"
    • Not "let me send a proposal" → "let me record a 5-minute audit"

    Don't say

    • "Can I have 15 minutes to walk you through our offerings?" — feels like a corporate pitch.
    • "We have a great team." — owners hire individuals they trust, not teams they've never met.
    • "Our process is..." — they don't care about process, they care about outcomes.
    • "ROI / KPIs / conversion rate optimization" — agency-jargon, instant signal they're being upsold.
    • "Six-month minimum" — kills the deal in 90% of cold calls. Always offer no-contract first; mention the longer terms ONLY after they say yes.
    • "We're better than [competitor]." — never name competitors. Position by what you do differently, not who you beat.
